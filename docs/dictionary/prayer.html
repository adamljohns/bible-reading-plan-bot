Prayer
/prɛr/
noun
Old French preiere → Medieval Latin precaria (obtained by entreaty) from Latin prex/precis (prayer, entreaty, request). Greek NT: proseuchē (προσευχή) — prayer specifically to God; also deēsis (δέησις) — petition, earnest request.

📖 Biblical Definition

Prayer is personal, relational communication with the living God — not a technique, ritual, or psychological exercise, but actual speech to an actual Person who hears and responds. The Psalms model the full range of prayer: adoration, confession, thanksgiving, supplication, lament, and praise. Jesus taught His disciples a pattern of prayer that prioritizes God's glory ("hallowed be your name"), aligns with God's will ("your kingdom come"), and then presents human needs (Matt. 6:9–13). Paul commands continuous prayer (1 Thess. 5:17) and teaches that the Holy Spirit intercedes for us when we lack words (Rom. 8:26). Prayer is the breath of spiritual life — evidence of living relationship with God.

• Matthew 6:9–13 — The Lord's Prayer: "Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name…"

• 1 Thessalonians 5:17 — "Pray without ceasing."

• Philippians 4:6 — "Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God."

• James 5:16 — "The prayer of a righteous person has great power as it is working."

• Romans 8:26 — "The Spirit himself intercedes for us with groanings too deep for words."

🔗 Greek & Hebrew Roots

G4335 — proseuchē (προσευχή): prayer, specifically to God. The most common NT word for prayer — always directed to God, always implying personal relationship.

G1162 — deēsis (δέησις): supplication, earnest petition, specific request. Used alongside proseuchē (Eph. 6:18) — the particular requests within a life of prayer.

H6419 — palal (פָּלַל): to pray, to intercede, to judge/mediate. The primary OT verb for prayer — includes the sense of making judgment or assessment before God.

H8605 — tepillah (תְּפִלָּה): prayer, intercession. The primary OT noun — appears in the superscriptions of several Psalms.

Usage

• "Daniel prayed three times a day even when it was a death sentence — prayer was not his emergency exit but his daily rhythm."

• "Jesus rose before dawn to pray in desolate places (Mark 1:35). If the Son of God needed prayer, the rest of us are fooling ourselves if we don't."

• "Prayer is not overcoming God's reluctance — it is laying hold of His willingness, aligning ourselves with what He has already purposed to do."

Related Words

Worship Faith Holy Hope Grace

Modern culture has converted prayer into either a therapeutic practice or a social gesture. "I'll pray for you" has largely become a polite way of expressing sympathy without commitment or expectation that anything will actually happen. New Age culture repackages prayer as "setting intentions," "manifesting," or "sending energy" — removing the Person from the practice and reducing it to a psychological technique. The "prosperity prayer" reduces intercession to a vending machine transaction: insert prayer, receive desired outcome. In progressive circles, corporate prayer is increasingly replaced by "mindfulness" as a generic spiritual practice untethered from the God of Scripture.
