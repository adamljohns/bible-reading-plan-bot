Redemption
/rɪˈdemp.ʃən/
noun
From Latin redemptio (buying back, ransom), from redimere (to buy back); re- + emere (to buy, take). Greek: apolytrōsis (ἀπολύτρωσις) — redemption, release by ransom. Hebrew: ga'al (גָּאַל) — to redeem, to act as kinsman-redeemer; padah (פָּדָה) — to ransom, redeem by payment.

📖 Biblical Definition

Redemption is the act of delivering someone from bondage by payment of a price. In Scripture it operates on multiple levels: Israel's physical redemption from Egypt (Exod 6:6) is the OT archetype; the cross is its ultimate fulfillment. The Greek apolytrōsis carries the image of the slave market — purchasing someone's freedom by paying the required price. Christ is our Redeemer who paid the ultimate ransom: "the Son of Man came…to give his life as a ransom for many" (Matt 20:28). The Hebrew go'el (kinsman-redeemer) adds relational depth: the redeemer must be a near kinsman, willing, and able to pay — all three fulfilled in Christ's incarnation and sacrifice. Redemption is not merely rescue; it is costly, substitutionary, and personal.

▶ 📜 Webster 1828 Definition

REDEMPTION, n. 1. Repurchase of captured goods or prisoners; the act of procuring the deliverance of persons or things from the possession and power of captors by the payment of an equivalent. 2. Deliverance from bondage, distress, or from the possession of another, by paying an equivalent. 3. In theology, the ransom or deliverance of sinners from the bondage of sin and the penalties of God's violated law by the atonement of Christ. The redemption of the world by our Savior is the source of unspeakable happiness to believers.

▶ ⚠️ Modern Corruption

Modern culture has sentimentalized redemption into personal recovery narratives — "redemption arcs" in film, athletes "redeeming themselves" with a good game, criminals "redeeming themselves" through good behavior. These uses strip the word of its transactional and substitutionary core. There is no cost, no redeemer, no bondage overcome — only personal improvement. In progressive theology, redemption is sometimes reframed as social liberation from systemic oppression, emptying it of its personal, spiritual, and substitutionary content. Biblical redemption requires a debt, a payment, a payer, and a price — none of which are optional metaphors.

📖 Key Scripture

• Ephesians 1:7 — "In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of our trespasses."

• Matthew 20:28 — "The Son of Man came…to give his life as a ransom for many."

• Galatians 3:13 — "Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law by becoming a curse for us."

• Ruth 4:4 — Boaz as kinsman-redeemer, the OT picture of Christ's redeeming love.

• 1 Peter 1:18–19 — "You were ransomed…not with perishable things…but with the precious blood of Christ."

🔗 Greek & Hebrew Roots

G629 — apolytrōsis (ἀπολύτρωσις): redemption, release by payment of ransom; appears 10 times in the NT.

G3083 — lytron (λύτρον): ransom, the price paid; used by Jesus in Matt 20:28 and Mark 10:45.

H1350 — ga'al (גָּאַל): to redeem as kinsman; the go'el is the kinsman-redeemer — Boaz for Ruth, God for Israel.

H6299 — padah (פָּדָה): to redeem by ransom payment; used of Israel's redemption from Egypt and firstborn consecration laws.
