Worship
/ˈwɜr.ʃɪp/
noun / verb
Old English weorthscipe: "worthship" — to ascribe worth to something. From weorth (worth, worthy) + -scipe (state of being). Worship is inherently a declaration: You are worthy.

📖 Biblical Definition

Worship is the total orientation of a creature toward its Creator — the ascribing of supreme worth to God through adoration, submission, obedience, and service. In Scripture, worship is not optional and not merely musical. Every human being worships something; the question is only what. True worship is "in spirit and truth" (John 4:24) — internal, authentic, and aligned with God's self-revelation. Jesus identified the greatest commandment as total love for God (Matt. 22:37), which is worship in its fullest form. Paul extended worship into all of life: "Present your bodies as a living sacrifice… this is your spiritual worship" (Rom. 12:1). Worship without obedience is not worship at all (Isa. 29:13).

John 4:23–24 — "But the hour is coming, and is now here, when the true worshipers will worship the Father in spirit and truth, for the Father is seeking such people to worship Him."

Romans 12:1 — "I appeal to you therefore, brothers, by the mercies of God, to present your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and acceptable to God, which is your spiritual worship."

Psalm 95:6 — "Oh come, let us worship and bow down; let us kneel before the LORD, our Maker!"

Revelation 4:11 — "Worthy are You, our Lord and God, to receive glory and honor and power, for You created all things."

Isaiah 29:13 — "This people draw near with their mouth and honor Me with their lips, while their hearts are far from Me."

🔗 Greek & Hebrew Roots

H7812 — shachah (שָׁחָה): to bow down, prostrate oneself; the primary OT word for worship — a physical posture expressing inward reverence.

G4352 — proskuneō (προσκυνέω): to bow before, to do reverence to; from pros (toward) + kuneō (to kiss) — worship as intimate approach and submission.

G3000 — latreuō (λατρεύω): to serve, to render religious service — worship expressed in devoted labor and service.

Usage

"Monday through Saturday is where most of your worship happens — in how you work, speak, and spend."

"The ancient Israelites were not called to find their own worship style; they were given a liturgy because God is the object, not the preference."

"Whatever you cannot stop thinking about, whatever shapes your decisions — that is what you worship."

Contemporary culture has reduced worship to a Sunday music set — evaluated by production quality, emotional experience, and personal preference. When churches ask "did you like the worship today?" they have already accepted the consumer model: the worshiper as audience, God as performer. Scripture's vision is the reverse. Further, culture worships openly but redirects toward self, celebrities, sports teams, and ideologies. When "worship" is reduced to a genre of Christian music, the word has been hollowed of its totalizing claim on human existence.

Old English weorðscipe ("worthiness, dignity, honor")
  → weorð ("worth, worthy") + -scipe ("-ship," state/condition)
    → Middle English worshipe → Modern English "worship"

Worship literally = "worth-ship" — ascribing worth to someone.
Note: "Your Worship" (addressing a judge/mayor) preserves the original meaning.

Greek:
προσκυνέω (proskyneō, G4352) — to worship, bow down, prostrate
  → πρός (toward) + κυνέω (to kiss) → literally "to kiss toward"
  → The image: bowing and kissing the feet or ground before a king
  → λατρεύω (latreuō, G3000) — to serve, render religious service/worship

Biblical parallel:
Proto-Semitic *šḥw → Hebrew שָׁחָה (shachah, H7812) — to bow down, worship, prostrate
  → The most common OT worship word — physical prostration before God
  → עָבַד (avad, H5647) — to serve, work (worship as service)
  → הָלַל (halal, H1984) — to praise, to shine, to boast in God
  → "Hallelujah" (hallelu-Yah) = "Praise the LORD" — from halal
