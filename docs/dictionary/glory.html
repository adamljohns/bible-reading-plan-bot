Glory
/ˈɡlɔːri/
noun / verb
Latin gloria (fame, renown, praise) → Old French gloire → English. Greek: doxa (δόξα) — glory, honor, reputation, splendor. Hebrew: kabod (כָּבוֹד) — weight, heaviness, honor, glory — from the idea of something weighty and substantial.

📖 Biblical Definition

The glory of God is the manifest weight of His full divine perfection — His holiness, power, beauty, righteousness, and majesty made visible and known. The Hebrew kabod suggests something of immense substance and worth — God's glory is the outward display of His infinite inner excellencies. It filled the tabernacle as a cloud (Ex. 40:34), blazed from Sinai (Ex. 24:17), and shone in the face of Jesus Christ (2 Cor. 4:6). All creation was made to display God's glory (Ps. 19:1). The chief end of humanity is to glorify God and enjoy Him forever. In eschatology, the whole earth will be filled with the knowledge of God's glory (Hab. 2:14).

• Psalm 19:1 — "The heavens declare the glory of God, and the sky above proclaims his handiwork."

• Isaiah 6:3 — "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts; the whole earth is full of his glory!"

• John 1:14 — "The Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father."

• Romans 3:23 — "All have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."

• Habakkuk 2:14 — "The earth will be filled with the knowledge of the glory of the LORD as the waters cover the sea."

🔗 Greek & Hebrew Roots

G1391 — doxa (δόξα): glory, honor, fame, splendor. In the NT, used primarily for the divine radiance and majesty that belongs to God and is shared with His people.

G1392 — doxazō (δοξάζω): to glorify, to praise, to honor. The verb — the act of ascribing glory to God or to Christ.

H3519 — kabod (כָּבוֹד): glory, honor, weight. The root implies substance and significance — God's glory has "weight" that everything else lacks.

Usage

• "The Shekinah glory that filled Solomon's temple was not decoration — it was the living, manifest presence of the God of the universe taking up residence among His people."

• "The Westminster Catechism's answer — 'Man's chief end is to glorify God and enjoy Him forever' — is the most counter-cultural sentence in human history."

• "Every human hunger for significance, beauty, and transcendence is a displaced longing for the glory of God — the one glory that will not leave us empty."

Related Words

Holy Worship Hope Grace Sanctification

Modern culture has redirected the concept of glory entirely toward the human. Social media is an engine for self-glorification — likes, followers, and viral moments constitute the modern pursuit of glory. Celebrity culture worships human achievement and appearance, turning athletes, entertainers, and influencers into objects of quasi-religious veneration. The phrase "giving yourself glory" is now culturally positive — the idea that a human being should be the center of their own narrative. In theology, prosperity gospel converts God's glory into a backdrop for human success, and anthropocentric worship focuses more on making humans feel significant than on honoring the infinite worth of God.

Latin gloria ("fame, renown, praise, glory")
  → Possibly PIE *gʷelH- ("to call out, cry aloud")
  → Old French gloire → Middle English glorie → Modern English "glory"

Latin cognates: glorify, glorious, inglorious, vainglory

Greek:
δόξα (doxa, G1391) — originally "opinion, expectation" in classical Greek
  → Radically transformed by the LXX to mean "glory, splendor, honor"
  → δοξάζω (doxazō) — to glorify, to honor
  → doxology (doxa + logos = "word of glory/praise")

Biblical parallel:
Proto-Semitic *kbd → Hebrew כָּבֵד (kaved, "to be heavy, weighty")
  → כָּבוֹד (kavod, H3519) — glory, honor, weight
  The glory of God = his "weight" — his substantial, undeniable presence
