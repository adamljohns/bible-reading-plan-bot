G156 · Greek · New Testament
αἰτία
aitia
Noun (feminine)
cause, reason, charge, accusation

Definition

The Greek noun aitia (αἰτία) means a cause, reason, or ground — and in legal contexts, a charge or accusation. It is the word used in trial narratives to describe the formal charge against a person. In the Passion narratives, it is used of the charge written above Jesus' cross.

Beyond legal usage, aitia can describe the reason or cause for something — why something is the case. In Matthew 19:3, the Pharisees ask Jesus about the 'cause' for divorce. In Acts 10:21, Cornelius' messengers explain their 'reason' for coming.

Usage & Theological Significance

The most theologically charged use of aitia in the Gospels is in the trial of Jesus. Pilate repeatedly declares, 'I find no aitia (charge/basis for accusation)' in Jesus (John 18:38; 19:4, 6). The irony is profound: the one man in history who was truly without sin, without legal fault, was executed on charges that even the Roman judge could not substantiate.

This is the heart of substitutionary atonement — Jesus bore the punishment for charges that rightly belonged to us. 2 Corinthians 5:21: 'God made him who had no sin to be sin for us.' The placard on the cross, the aitia nailed above Him, was technically false by Roman legal standards — but spiritually it was the most accurate description of what was happening: the Righteous One bearing the charge of the unrighteous.
