H3001 · Hebrew · Old Testament
יָבֵשׁ
Yabesh
Verb
To be dry, wither, be ashamed

Definition

The Hebrew verb yabesh (יָבֵשׁ) means to be dry, to dry up, or to wither — used of dried-up water, withered plants, and dying bones. It also carries the metaphorical sense of being ashamed or confounded. The related noun yabbesheth refers to dry land. The word encompasses both physical aridity and the spiritual dryness of shame and desolation.

Usage & Theological Significance

The vision of the valley of dry bones (Ezekiel 37) is the most powerful use of yabesh — the bones are very dry (yabeshim meod), representing Israel's utter spiritual and national desolation. Yet God breathes life into what is parched and dead, revealing that He specializes in reviving what is withered. Isaiah 40:7–8 contrasts the withering grass (humanity) with the enduring word of God. The Psalms use dryness to describe spiritual desolation (Psalm 22:15; 102:4), and Christ on the cross fulfilled the imagery: 'My mouth is dried up like a potsherd' (Psalm 22:15).

Key Bible Verses

Ezekiel 37:4 Then he said to me, 'Prophesy to these bones and say to them: Dry bones, hear the word of the LORD!'
Isaiah 40:7 The grass withers and the flowers fall, because the breath of the LORD blows on them.
Psalm 22:15 My mouth is dried up like a potsherd, and my tongue sticks to the roof of my mouth.
James 1:11 For the sun rises with scorching heat and withers the plant; its blossom falls and its beauty is destroyed.
Nahum 1:4 He rebukes the sea and dries it up; he makes all the rivers run dry.
