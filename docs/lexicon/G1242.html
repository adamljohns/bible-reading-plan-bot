G1242 · Greek · New Testament
διαθήκη
Diatheke
Noun feminine
covenant / testament / will

Definition

Diatheke (διαθήκη) means a legally binding arrangement — specifically a will or testament (a unilateral agreement established by one party, not negotiated bilaterally as in a contract). In the LXX (Septuagint), it translates the Hebrew berit (covenant). The very word 'Testament' in 'New Testament' comes from the Latin testamentum translating diatheke.

Usage & Theological Significance

The covenant theology of Scripture reaches its summit in diatheke. The 'new covenant' (diatheke kaine) promised in Jeremiah 31:31-34 is quoted in full in Hebrews 8:8-12 — the longest OT quotation in the NT. Jesus instituted it at the Last Supper: 'This cup is the new covenant (diatheke) in my blood' (1 Corinthians 11:25; Luke 22:20). Hebrews 9:15-17 makes the will/testament analogy explicit: a diatheke only takes effect at the death of the testator. Christ had to die for the New Covenant to take effect — which is precisely what the cross accomplished. All of God's redemptive promises are structured as diatheke — unilateral, grace-based commitments that cannot be annulled (Galatians 3:15).

Key Bible Verses

Matthew 26:28 'For this is my blood of the covenant (diatheke), which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins.'
Hebrews 9:15 Therefore he is the mediator of a new covenant, so that those who are called may receive the promised eternal inheritance.
Galatians 3:15 Brothers, I speak in human terms: even with a man-made covenant, no one annuls it or adds to it once it has been ratified.
Hebrews 8:6 But as it is, Christ has obtained a ministry that is as much more excellent than the old as the covenant he mediates is better.
Jeremiah 31:31 'Behold, the days are coming, declares the LORD, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and the house of Judah.'

Word Study

Every act of Christian worship — especially the Lord's Supper — is a covenant renewal act. Diatheke frames the entire Bible: from the Noahic covenant (Genesis 9), to the Abrahamic (Genesis 15, 17), to the Mosaic (Exodus 19-24), to the Davidic (2 Samuel 7), to the New (Jeremiah 31 / Luke 22). Each covenant builds on the former and anticipates the next — all fulfilled and consummated in Christ. The phrase 'New Testament' is not a book title but a reality claim: we live in the age of the New Covenant.
