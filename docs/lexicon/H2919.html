H2919 · Hebrew · Old Testament
טַל
Tal
Noun, masculine
Dew

Definition

The Hebrew word tal refers to dew — the moisture that condenses from the atmosphere and falls silently in the night, refreshing the earth by morning. In the arid climate of the ancient Near East, dew was not merely a meteorological phenomenon but a life-giving gift. Israel's agricultural survival often depended on it (cf. Zechariah 8:12). Tal appears approximately 35 times in the Old Testament, almost always as a positive image of blessing, life, and divine refreshment.

Usage & Theological Significance

Tal (dew) is a rich symbol of God's grace and renewal. It came silently in the night — like the manna it accompanied (Numbers 11:9), like the whisper of God to Elijah (1 Kings 19:12), like the incarnation of Christ arriving quietly in the darkness of human history. Hosea uses dew as the image of God's promised restoration: I will be like the dew to Israel; he will blossom like a lily (Hosea 14:5). Isaiah uses it as a metaphor for resurrection: Your dew is a dew of light, and the earth will give birth to its dead (Isaiah 26:19). The Holy Spirit falls like dew — quietly, life-givingly, before you even know it.

Key Bible Verses
