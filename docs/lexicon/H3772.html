H3772 · Hebrew · Old Testament
כָּרַת
karath
Verb
to cut off, to cut down; to make a covenant

Definition

The verb karath primarily means to cut — to cut down a tree, cut off a person, or cut through something. Its most theologically significant idiom is karath berit — literally 'to cut a covenant.' The phrase recalls ancient covenant-making rites in which animals were cut in two and the parties walked between the pieces, invoking a self-curse: 'May this be done to me if I break this covenant.'

Usage & Theological Significance

Genesis 15 is the foundational covenant text: God commands Abraham to cut animals in half, then passes between them as a smoking firepot and blazing torch — alone, while Abraham sleeps. This is not a bilateral agreement; it is God binding Himself unilaterally to His promises. Every covenant in Scripture — Noahic, Mosaic, Davidic, New Covenant — is a karath berit. Jeremiah 31:31-33 announces the coming of a New Covenant (berit chadashah): God will cut a new covenant not like the Mosaic one, writing the law on hearts rather than stone. The Epistle to the Hebrews reveals that this New Covenant is ratified by Christ's body, cut on the cross — the ultimate karath berit, sealed in His own blood.

Key Bible Verses
