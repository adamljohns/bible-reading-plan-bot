H1350 · Hebrew · Old Testament
גָּאַל
Gaal
Verb (Qal, Niphal, Hiphil)
Redeem, Kinsman-Redeemer, Avenge

Definition

The Hebrew verb gāʾal (גָּאַל) means to redeem, act as kinsman-redeemer, or avenge blood. The goʾel (redeemer) was a family member who had the duty and right to buy back a relative who had fallen into slavery, to reclaim ancestral land sold under duress, and to avenge the blood of a slain relative. This legal institution is foundational to understanding biblical redemption.

Usage & Theological Significance

Gāʾal is one of the Old Testament's most theologically freighted words. God Himself is described as Israel's Goʾel — their divine Kinsman-Redeemer — most powerfully in Isaiah 40–55, where the title appears at least 13 times (Isaiah 41:14; 43:14; 44:6; 47:4; 48:17; 49:7, 26; 54:5, 8). The book of Ruth is the narrative embodiment of this institution: Boaz's redemption of Ruth and Naomi's land is the theological backdrop for the entire messianic lineage. Jesus Christ fulfills the role of the ultimate Goʾel — becoming our kinsman through incarnation, then paying our redemption price through His blood (Galatians 4:4–5; Revelation 5:9).
