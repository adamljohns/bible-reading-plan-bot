H3588 · Hebrew · Old Testament
כִּי
ki
Conjunction
for, because, when, that, indeed, surely

Definition

Ki is one of the most versatile and frequently used words in biblical Hebrew, appearing over 4,400 times. It introduces causal clauses ("because, for"), temporal clauses ("when"), content clauses ("that"), conditional clauses ("if"), and emphatic assertions ("indeed, surely").

Like gar in Greek, ki is the connective tissue of Hebrew thought. Understanding which sense is operative in any passage — causal, temporal, content, emphatic — is crucial to accurate interpretation.

Usage & Theological Significance

Some of the most theologically rich statements in the OT hinge on ki. Psalm 23:4 — "I will fear no evil, for (ki) you are with me" — the ki is the reason for fearlessness: God's presence. The logic of faith is always a ki.

Isaiah's "fear not" oracles pivot on ki: "Fear not, for (ki) I have redeemed you; I have called you by name; you are mine" (Isaiah 43:1). The ki grounds the command not to fear in the fact of redemption. Commands are always grounded in realities.

The causal ki in Lamentations and the Psalms often introduces divine character as the ground for praise: "Praise the LORD, for (ki) he is good; for (ki) his steadfast love endures forever" (Psalm 118:1). The theology of worship is always ki-shaped — worship because of who God is and what He has done.

Key Bible Verses

Isaiah 43:1 Do not fear, for (ki) I have redeemed you; I have summoned you by name; you are mine.
Psalm 118:1 Give thanks to the LORD, for (ki) he is good; for (ki) his love endures forever.
Genesis 1:4 God saw that the light was good (ki tov — literally, "for it was good").
Deuteronomy 7:9 Know therefore that (ki) the LORD your God is God; he is the faithful God.
