H2930 · Hebrew · Old Testament
טָמֵא
tame
Verb (Qal)
be unclean / become defiled / be impure

Definition

Tame (טָמֵא) means to be unclean or impure — a state that excludes one from the holy assembly and from approaching God's presence. Levitical purity law uses this word hundreds of times to describe states requiring separation, waiting, and cleansing. Uncleanness could arise from: contact with a corpse, skin diseases, bodily discharges, contact with certain animals, or moral sin.

Usage & Theological Significance

The theology of tame reveals God's absolute holiness — His presence is so pure that anything contaminated cannot approach Him without being consumed. The system of purity laws taught Israel that sin has consequences that require atonement, not just declaration. The Levitical priesthood managed the boundary between clean and unclean, holy and common (Leviticus 10:10). Isaiah's cry 'I am a man of unclean lips' (Isaiah 6:5) uses this exact framework — confronting God's holiness produces recognition of defilement. The ultimate answer to human uncleanness is not ritual but the blood of Christ: 'The blood of Jesus his Son cleanses us from all sin' (1 John 1:7).

Key Bible Verses

Leviticus 11:44 'I am the LORD your God. Consecrate yourselves therefore, and be holy, for I am holy. You shall not defile yourselves with any swarming creature.'
Isaiah 6:5 And I said: 'Woe is me! For I am lost; for I am a man of unclean lips, and I dwell in the midst of a people of unclean lips; for my eyes have seen the King, the LORD of hosts!'
Numbers 5:3 You shall put out both male and female, putting them outside the camp, that they may not defile their camp, in the midst of which I dwell.
Lamentations 4:15 People cried at them, 'Away! Unclean!' 'Away! Away! Do not touch!' So they became fugitives and wanderers.
Mark 1:41 Moved with pity, he stretched out his hand and touched him and said to him, 'I will; be clean.' And immediately the leprosy left him.

Word Study

The clean/unclean distinction runs through all of Scripture. The Levitical system educated Israel in the holiness of God and the seriousness of sin. Jesus's healings often involved touching 'unclean' persons (lepers, the dead, the woman with a bleeding disorder) — not to become unclean Himself, but to cleanse them. This inversion of purity logic — holiness overcomes uncleanness rather than being contaminated by it — is the heart of the incarnation. Christ absorbed our uncleanness at the cross (2 Corinthians 5:21).
