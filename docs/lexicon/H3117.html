H3117 · Hebrew · Old Testament
יוֹם
yom
Noun (masculine)
day, time, season

Definition

The Hebrew yom is the most common word for day in the Old Testament, occurring over 2,300 times. It carries a range of meanings: a single day (from sundown to sundown in Hebrew reckoning), a period of time, or even an era or epoch.

Context determines its meaning. In Genesis 1, the phrase erev va-boqer ("evening and morning") bounds each yom, suggesting a structured 24-hour period. Elsewhere, as in "the day of the Lord" (yom YHWH), it denotes a sovereign divine moment of judgment and redemption.

Usage & Theological Significance

The yom YHWH — the Day of the Lord — is one of the most potent theological concepts in the Hebrew prophets. It is simultaneously a day of darkness for the wicked (Amos 5:18–20) and a day of salvation for the remnant (Joel 2:28–32).

God is the sovereign of time. Every day is His gift. Psalm 118:24 declares, "This is the day the LORD has made; let us rejoice and be glad in it." The Sabbath (Shabbat) sanctified the seventh day, teaching Israel that time itself belongs to the Creator.

In eschatology, yom is used for the final decisive intervention of God in history — the day of resurrection, judgment, and new creation. The New Testament echoes this in "the last day" (John 6:39–44).

Key Bible Verses

Genesis 1:5 God called the light day, and the darkness he called night. And there was evening, and there was morning — the first day.
Psalm 118:24 This is the day the LORD has made; we will rejoice and be glad in it.
Isaiah 2:12 The LORD Almighty has a day in store for all the proud and lofty.
Amos 5:18 Woe to you who long for the day of the LORD! That day will be darkness, not light.
Lamentations 3:22–23 His mercies are new every morning — yom by yom; great is your faithfulness.
