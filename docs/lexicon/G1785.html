G1785 · Greek · New Testament
ἐντολή
entolē
Noun, feminine
Commandment, order, instruction

Definition

From entellomai (to command, charge), denoting an authoritative command, injunction, or precept. Used for the Mosaic commandments, Jesus' 'new commandment,' and apostolic instructions. It emphasizes the authoritative source of the instruction — a commandment derives its force from the one who issues it.

Usage & Theological Significance

Jesus distilled the Torah into two great commandments (entolai): love God and love neighbor (Matt 22:36-40). He then gave a 'new commandment' (entolēn kainēn): 'Love one another; as I have loved you' (John 13:34). The 'newness' is not the command to love (that was in Leviticus) but the standard: 'as I have loved you' — sacrificial, cross-shaped love. John's theology makes commandment-keeping the evidence of love: 'If ye love me, keep my commandments' (John 14:15); 'This is the love of God, that we keep his commandments: and his commandments are not grievous' (1 John 5:3). Obedience and love are not opponents but partners — entolē is the shape love takes when it acts.
