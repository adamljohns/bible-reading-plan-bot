H6754 · Hebrew · Old Testament
צֶלֶם
Tselem
Noun, masculine
Image / Likeness / Idol

Definition

The Hebrew noun tselem (צֶלֶם) means image, likeness, or representation. In its most theologically charged use, it appears in Genesis 1:26–27: God created humanity betselem Elohim — in the image of God (imago Dei). It also describes idols and carved images of pagan gods (Numbers 33:52; 2 Kings 11:18). The word implies something that resembles and represents an original — a copy that carries the identity of its prototype. In the ancient Near East, a king's image (tselem) was erected in conquered territories to represent his rule and authority.

Usage & Theological Significance

The imago Dei — humanity as God's tselem — is foundational to Christian anthropology and ethics. It means human beings are created to represent God in creation, to act as His vice-regents over the earth (Genesis 1:28). The image was not destroyed by the Fall but was marred and distorted (Genesis 5:3 — Adam's son Seth was born "in his own likeness, in his own image," pointing to inherited falleness). The New Testament presents Christ as the perfect image of God (eikon tou Theou, 2 Corinthians 4:4; Colossians 1:15), and redemption as the restoration of God's image in believers (Colossians 3:10; Romans 8:29). The imago Dei grounds the dignity of every human life (Genesis 9:6) and the mission of the church to display God's character to the world.
