H2600 · Hebrew · Old Testament
חִנָּם
Chinnam
Adverb
Without cause, freely, for nothing

Definition

The Hebrew adverb chinnam (חִנָּם) derives from chen (grace, favor) and means freely, gratis, without payment, or without cause. It is used both for receiving something without payment (freely given) and for acting without just cause (undeservedly, unjustly). The context determines whether the freeness is positive (grace) or negative (unjust).

Usage & Theological Significance

Chinnam appears in Isaiah 52:3 — 'You were sold for nothing, and without money you will be redeemed' — a stunning declaration that Israel's redemption would be a free act of grace, not purchased by human merit. In Isaiah 55:1, God invites all who are thirsty to come and eat without money and without cost. This freeness of grace is foundational to the gospel: salvation cannot be earned; it is given chinnam. Conversely, when Job is persecuted without cause (Job 2:3), the word underscores injustice — Christ too suffered without cause (Psalm 35:19; John 15:25).

Key Bible Verses

Isaiah 52:3 For this is what the LORD says: 'You were sold for nothing, and without money you will be redeemed.'
Isaiah 55:1 Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters; and you who have no money, come, buy and eat! Come, buy wine and milk without money and without cost.
Job 2:3 And he still maintains his integrity, though you incited me against him to ruin him without any reason.
Psalm 35:19 Do not let those who are my enemies without cause gloat over me; do not let those who hate me without reason maliciously wink the eye.
2 Samuel 24:24 I will not sacrifice to the LORD my God burnt offerings that cost me nothing.
