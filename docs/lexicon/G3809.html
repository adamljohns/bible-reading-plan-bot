G3809 · Greek · New Testament
παιδεία
paideia
Noun, feminine
Training, discipline, instruction, correction

Definition

A Greek noun meaning training, education, discipline, correction, instruction, chastening. From pais (child). In classical Greek it referred to the whole process of educating a child — intellectual, moral, and physical formation. In biblical usage it emphasizes formative discipline — training through correction, not merely academic instruction.

Usage & Theological Significance

Hebrews 12:5-11 develops the theology of paideia most fully: God disciplines (paideuei) those He loves, as a father disciplines his son. This discipline is 'painful rather than pleasant,' but 'yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness.' The implication is profound: suffering under God's hand is not punishment but parenting. Paul uses paideia in Ephesians 6:4: 'Bring them up in the discipline and instruction of the Lord' — parents are to form their children as God forms His. In 2 Timothy 3:16, Scripture is profitable for paideia in righteousness — the Bible itself is a training program. The word bridges discipline and education, correcting the modern tendency to separate love from correction.

Key Bible Verses

Hebrews 12:7 It is for discipline that you have to endure. God is treating you as sons. For what son is there whom his father does not discipline?
Hebrews 12:11 For the moment all discipline seems painful rather than pleasant, but later it yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness.
Ephesians 6:4 Fathers, do not provoke your children to anger, but bring them up in the discipline and instruction of the Lord.
2 Timothy 3:16 All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness.
Proverbs 22:15 (LXX) Folly is bound up in the heart of a child, but the rod of discipline drives it far from him.
