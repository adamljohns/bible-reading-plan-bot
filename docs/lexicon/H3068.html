H3068 · Hebrew · Old Testament
יְהוָה
YHWH (Yahweh)
Proper noun
LORD, the proper name of God

Definition

The personal, covenantal name of God, occurring over 6,800 times in the Old Testament — making it the most frequent proper noun in the Hebrew Bible. Known as the Tetragrammaton (four letters: yod-he-vav-he), this name was revealed to Moses at the burning bush (Exodus 3:14–15). Its exact pronunciation was lost when Jewish tradition stopped speaking it aloud out of reverence, substituting Adonai ("Lord").

Usage & Theological Significance

While Elohim emphasizes God's power and transcendence, YHWH is the name of relationship, covenant, and personal presence. God introduces Himself as YHWH when making promises, entering covenant, and acting in salvation history. The name is connected to the Hebrew verb hayah ("to be"), suggesting God's self-existence and eternal nature — "I AM WHO I AM" (Exodus 3:14). In English Bibles, YHWH is typically rendered as "LORD" (all capitals).

Key Bible Verses

Exodus 3:14-15 God said to Moses, 'I AM WHO I AM... The LORD [YHWH], the God of your fathers... this is my name forever.'
Psalm 23:1 The LORD [YHWH] is my shepherd; I shall not want.
Isaiah 42:8 I am the LORD [YHWH]; that is my name! I will not yield my glory to another.
Deuteronomy 6:4 Hear, O Israel: The LORD [YHWH] our God, the LORD [YHWH] is one.
Exodus 34:6 The LORD [YHWH], the LORD [YHWH], the compassionate and gracious God.

Word Study

The revelation of the divine name is progressive: the patriarchs knew God as El Shaddai, but the full significance of YHWH was revealed to Moses (Exodus 6:3). The compound names (YHWH-Jireh, YHWH-Rapha, YHWH-Nissi) reveal different facets of His covenantal character. In the New Testament, Jesus' statement "Before Abraham was, I AM" (John 8:58) is widely understood as a direct claim to the divine name.
