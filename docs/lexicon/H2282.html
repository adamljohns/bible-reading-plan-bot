H2282 · Hebrew · Old Testament
חַג
Chag
Noun, masculine
Festival / Feast / Pilgrimage

Definition

The Hebrew word chag refers to a sacred festival or feast, typically involving pilgrimage to a central sanctuary. The root may be related to an Arabic word meaning 'to circle or dance,' reflecting the celebratory processions involved. In the Old Testament, the three major chagim (plural) are: Pesach (Passover/Unleavened Bread), Shavuot (Feast of Weeks/Pentecost), and Sukkot (Feast of Tabernacles/Booths). Every male Israelite was required to appear before God at these three feasts each year (Deuteronomy 16:16).

Usage & Theological Significance

The chagim were not merely cultural celebrations — they were rehearsals of redemption. Passover commemorated the Exodus and prefigured Christ's death (1 Corinthians 5:7). Pentecost commemorated the giving of the Torah and was fulfilled by the giving of the Holy Spirit (Acts 2). Tabernacles celebrated God's provision in the wilderness and foreshadows the eternal dwelling of God with His people (Revelation 21:3). Paul wrote that the feasts are a shadow of the things to come, but the substance belongs to Christ (Colossians 2:17). The church still lives in the chag — awaiting the final fulfillment.
