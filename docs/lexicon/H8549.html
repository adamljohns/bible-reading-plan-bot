H8549 · Hebrew · Old Testament
תָּמִים
tamim
Adjective
blameless, complete, without blemish

Definition

A Hebrew adjective meaning complete, whole, blameless, or without blemish, appearing about 90 times. Tamim describes both moral integrity and physical perfection. Sacrificial animals must be tamim — without defect (Lev 1:3). Covenant partners are called to be tamim — walking blamelessly before God (Gen 17:1).

Usage & Theological Significance

God commands Abraham: 'Walk before me, and be blameless' (Gen 17:1). Noah was tamim in his generation (Gen 6:9) — not sinless, but marked by integrity. The tamim person is wholehearted, undivided, living in covenant faithfulness. Job is described as tamim (Job 1:1) — his integrity is the foundation of the book's drama.

Key Bible Verses

Genesis 17:1 Walk before me, and be blameless.
Deuteronomy 18:13 You shall be blameless before the LORD your God.
Psalm 18:30 This God—his way is perfect; the word of the LORD proves true.
Job 1:1 That man was blameless and upright.
Leviticus 1:3 He shall offer a male without blemish.

Word Study

The root t-m-m conveys completeness and wholeness. Tamim is not perfection in the sense of flawlessness but wholeness in the sense of integrity — one's life matches one's profession. The word applies to God's way (Ps 18:30), God's work (Deut 32:4), and to those who walk in His covenant. The NT concept of teleios (mature, complete) in Matt 5:48 echoes tamim.
