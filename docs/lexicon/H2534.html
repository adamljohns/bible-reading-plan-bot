H2534 · Hebrew · Old Testament
חֵמָה
chēmâ
Noun, feminine
Wrath, fury, hot anger; venom

Definition

Derived from yācham (to be hot), this word denotes intense heat — both literal (venom, poison) and metaphorical (burning anger, fury). It describes the most intense degree of divine or human wrath — not cold displeasure but white-hot fury. The same word is used for serpent venom (Deut 32:24; Ps 58:4), connecting wrath and poison through the image of deadly heat.

Usage & Theological Significance

Divine chēmâ is God's holy response to covenant violation and injustice. It 'burns' and is 'poured out' like molten fire (Jer 7:20; Ezek 22:22), imagery that emphasizes its overwhelming, consuming nature. Yet divine wrath is never capricious — it is the necessary expression of God's holiness encountering sin. The prophets hold wrath and mercy in tension: God's fury falls, yet He 'will not keep his anger forever' (Jer 3:12). Isaiah prophesies a servant who will drink the 'cup of his fury' on behalf of others (Isa 51:17, 22), pointing to the cross where Christ absorbed divine chēmâ. The eschatological 'winepress of the fury of the wrath of God Almighty' (Rev 19:15) draws on this Hebrew concept of holy, burning judgment against evil.
