H3384 · Hebrew · Old Testament
יָרָה
yarah
Verb
To throw, shoot, instruct, teach

Definition

A Hebrew verb with two primary meanings: (1) to throw, cast, shoot (as an arrow); and (2) to instruct, teach, direct, point out. The Hiphil form means 'to teach, guide, direct.' This is the verb from which Torah is derived — Torah literally means 'instruction' or 'direction' from this root.

Usage & Theological Significance

The connection between yarah (to point/direct) and Torah (instruction/law) reveals that God's Law is not arbitrary rule-making but directional guidance — like an arrow pointed at a target. Torah is God showing His people which direction to walk. The 'early rain' (yoreh, from the same root) that waters the land becomes a metaphor for divine teaching that causes spiritual growth (Joel 2:23). When Hosea 6:3 promises God will 'come to us like the rain,' it evokes this teaching-rain connection. The priest's role was to yarah — to instruct the people in God's ways (Deuteronomy 33:10), making teaching a sacred, priestly vocation.

Key Bible Verses

Deuteronomy 33:10 They shall teach Jacob your rules and Israel your law.
Exodus 4:12 Now therefore go, and I will be with your mouth and teach you what you shall speak.
2 Kings 17:27 Send there one of the priests whom you carried away, and let him go and dwell there and teach them the law of the god of the land.
Proverbs 4:4 He taught me and said to me, 'Let your heart hold fast my words; keep my commandments, and live.'
Joel 2:23 Be glad, O children of Zion, and rejoice in the LORD your God, for he has given the early rain for your vindication.
