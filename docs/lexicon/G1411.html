G1411 · Greek · New Testament
δύναμις
dynamis
Noun, feminine
power, might, miracle

Definition

The Greek word for power, might, ability, or miracle, appearing 120 times. It is the root of English "dynamite" and "dynamic." Dynamis refers to inherent power — the actual ability to accomplish something. It is the power of God at work: in creation, in miracles, in the gospel, and in the resurrection.

Usage & Theological Significance

Paul calls the gospel "the dynamis of God for salvation" (Romans 1:16) — not just a message ABOUT power but a message that IS power. The Holy Spirit is the dynamis that empowers believers (Acts 1:8). Christ's resurrection is the supreme display of divine dynamis (Ephesians 1:19–20). Paul's paradox: God's dynamis is made perfect in weakness (2 Corinthians 12:9).

Key Bible Verses

Acts 1:8 You will receive power [dynamis] when the Holy Spirit comes on you.
Romans 1:16 The gospel... is the power [dynamis] of God that brings salvation.
Ephesians 1:19-20 His incomparably great power [dynamis] for us who believe — the same power he exerted when he raised Christ from the dead.
2 Corinthians 12:9 My power [dynamis] is made perfect in weakness.
1 Corinthians 1:18 The message of the cross... is the power [dynamis] of God.

Word Study

The distinction between dynamis (inherent power/ability) and exousia (G1849, authorized power/right) is important: Jesus had both — the inherent power to perform miracles AND the authorized right to forgive sins. Dynamis also means "miracle" ("mighty works" — Matthew 11:20), revealing that miracles are not violations of natural law but displays of a higher power.
