G1344 · Greek · New Testament
δικαιόω
dikaioō
Verb
to justify, declare righteous, acquit, vindicate

Definition

Dikaioō (G1344) means to justify, to declare righteous, to acquit. It is the forensic-legal verb at the heart of Pauline soteriology. It does not mean 'to make righteous' in the sense of moral transformation (that is sanctification) but rather 'to declare or pronounce righteous' — a judicial declaration of right standing before God. It appears approximately 40 times in the New Testament, concentrated heavily in Romans and Galatians.

Usage & Theological Significance

Justification (dikaiōsis, dikaioō) is one of the central doctrines of the Protestant Reformation and of Pauline theology. The critical question of Romans — 'How can a sinful person stand righteous before a holy God?' — is answered through dikaioō: God declares the ungodly righteous through faith in Christ (Romans 4:5).

This is a forensic (courtroom) metaphor: God is the judge, the sinner stands guilty, Christ takes the penalty, and God pronounces 'not guilty' — dikaiōtheis. The ground of justification is not human merit but Christ's righteousness imputed (credited) to the believer. 'We hold that one is justified by faith apart from works of the law' (Romans 3:28). This declaration is instantaneous and complete — unlike sanctification, which is progressive. The justified person stands before God as if they had never sinned.

Key Bible Verses

Romans 3:24 ...and are justified by his grace as a gift, through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus.
Romans 4:5 And to the one who does not work but believes in him who justifies the ungodly, his faith is counted as righteousness.
Galatians 2:16 ...yet we know that a person is not justified by works of the law but through faith in Jesus Christ.
Romans 5:1 Therefore, since we have been justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ.
1 Corinthians 6:11 But you were washed, you were sanctified, you were justified in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ.
