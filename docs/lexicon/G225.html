G225 · Greek · New Testament
ἀλήθεια
alētheia
Noun, feminine
truth, reality

Definition

The Greek word for truth, 109 times. Etymologically "un-hiddenness" — truth as reality unveiled. The NT combines this Greek philosophical sense with Hebrew emeth (H571) — truth as reliability and faithfulness. The result: truth that is both ontological (what IS real) and personal (WHO is trustworthy).

Usage & Theological Significance

Jesus' claim "I am the truth" (John 14:6) is radical — He doesn't merely teach truth, He IS truth. The Spirit is "the Spirit of truth" guiding believers into all truth (John 16:13). Paul speaks of "the truth of the gospel" as non-negotiable. In John, truth is linked with freedom (John 8:32).

Key Bible Verses

John 14:6 'I am the way and the truth [alētheia] and the life.'
John 8:32 You will know the truth [alētheia], and the truth will set you free.
John 17:17 Sanctify them by the truth [alētheia]; your word is truth.
Ephesians 4:15 Speaking the truth [alētheia] in love.
3 John 1:4 I have no greater joy than to hear my children are walking in the truth [alētheia].

Word Study

John's Gospel uses alētheia 25 times — making truth a central theme. Jesus is truth (14:6), the Spirit leads into truth (16:13), God's word IS truth (17:17), truth sets free (8:32). Greek philosophy (Plato's forms as reality) and Hebrew tradition (emeth as faithfulness) converge in Jesus as truth incarnate.
